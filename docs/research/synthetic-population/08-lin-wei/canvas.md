# Living Canvas: Lin Wei

**Portland, Oregon | March 2026**

---

## Essence Core

**Lin Wei** -- Southeast Portland, Oregon

*The person who sees how everything connects to food — and is ready to stop circling and start building.*

Lin grew up inside a food business he didn't choose, absorbing twenty years of knowledge about how food feeds people. He left that kitchen to study food systems from the outside, and now he's finding his way back in — on his own terms. He sees connections other people miss: coffee waste to compost, empty lots to gardens, neighborhoods to supply chains. He pulls people into things without trying. His body is strong, his joy is real, his conviction runs deep. What he's been missing is the discipline to stay with one expression of it long enough for it to become his.

The scatteredness isn't a flaw. It's a systems thinker without a system to tend. This canvas gives him one.

## Situation Portrait: 8 Dimensions

### What's Strong

**Body (4):** Lin's physical infrastructure is exceptional for a 23-year-old. He bikes everywhere — commutes, errands, the Springwater Corridor all the way to Boring when he needs to process something. He cooks real food nearly every night. He's energetic, capable of physical labor, and his body is genuinely the engine that runs everything else. This dimension needs protection, not improvement.

**Joy (4):** Lin enjoys his life. Cooking with friends, bike rides, the community garden, reading Fukuoka on the porch. The joy isn't aspirational — it's present. Time disappears when he's learning soil from Pete or making a complicated meal with music on. The risk is that joy gets sacrificed to a career panic that doesn't actually exist. His life is good. The direction is missing, not the living.

**Growth (4):** His brain is hungry and he's feeding it — regenerative agriculture, food systems theory, climate science. The intellectual energy is enormous. The gap is between theoretical knowledge (strong) and practical skill (beginner). He can talk about soil biology but can't yet read soil by feel without Pete's help. The farm internship closes this gap directly.

### What's Under Pressure

**Identity (3):** Lin is caught between his parents' story and his own. He respects what they built — a successful restaurant from nothing — but he defines himself partly in opposition to it. "Environmental studies kid" and "my parents' son" are both labels he's outgrowing, and nothing has replaced them yet. Identity solidifies through action, not reflection. Building something specific gives him a name for who he is.

**Money (3):** $2,200/month income, $850 rent, $24K student loans, $200/month cushion, $800 in checking. Functional but fragile. No savings buffer. The farm internship will compress this further ($1,600-1,800/month total income during apprenticeship months). Income-based repayment on loans is necessary. The financial situation is manageable but requires attention and can't absorb surprises.

**People (3):** Good roommates, a solid friend group, mentors he hasn't fully engaged. Pete (organic farmer, community garden), Dani (People's Food Co-op), and his mother (twenty years of food business knowledge) represent a curriculum sitting in his relationships. The gap: Lin hasn't asked for help because asking feels like admitting he needs it, which in his family means admitting he's wrong. Activating these relationships is a leverage point.

### What's Neglected

**Purpose (2):** This is the core gap. Lin has conviction — food systems, climate, community food access — but no direction. He applied to six things and finished two. The energy scatters because no single path has become specific enough to hold it. Purpose at 2 doesn't mean he lacks values. It means he hasn't channeled them into anything sustained. The farm internship is the purpose test.

**Home (2):** A shared house in a gentrifying neighborhood, a small room, no outdoor space except a 4x8 community garden bed six blocks away. The living situation works but offers no room to grow and faces economic pressure from rising rents. Home isn't a crisis, but it's not a foundation either. It's a holding pattern.

### The Leverage Point

**One commitment held for ninety days.** If Lin completes the farm internship application and starts the apprenticeship, everything else organizes around it: learning becomes focused, mentors become useful, identity shifts from "figuring it out" to "building a specific thing," and the follow-through muscle develops through repetition rather than willpower. The commitment itself is the intervention.

## Enterprise Stack

### Enterprise 1: Current Employment — The Financial Floor
**Role: Foundation**

The barista job at the coffee shop. $2,200/month, morning-heavy schedule, predictable. It pays rent and keeps the lights on. Not where Lin wants to be, but it's stable and leaves afternoons free.

**The upgrade path:** Apply to the People's Food Co-op when a position opens. Dani has already invited him. Co-op work pays comparably to barista work but teaches inventory, sourcing, community food infrastructure, and cooperative governance — all skills that matter for where Lin is going. If the co-op can match his current pay, it's a lateral move that transforms what his workday teaches him. If it can't, keep the coffee shop and volunteer at the co-op one shift a week.

- Time: 20-30 hours/week (current). Shifts to 12-15 hours/week during farm season (afternoon shifts only).
- Income: $2,200/month full-time. $1,200-1,400/month on reduced hours during apprenticeship.
- Capital built: Financial (income floor), Social (co-op community), Intellectual (food system operations).
- QoL check: Afternoons/evenings stay free. Body stays active via bike commute.

### Enterprise 2: Farm Apprenticeship — The Learning Enterprise
**Role: Anchor**

Spring-through-fall apprenticeship at an organic farm near Canby, Oregon. Four days a week, 7am to 1pm. $400/month stipend plus lunch. This is where Lin tests whether growing food is the center of his work or the foundation under something else.

**Why this enterprise, for this person:** Lin described the farm visit as the one time he forgot his phone existed. His body can handle it — he bikes to Boring for fun. His intellectual capital in food systems theory is high (4) but his experiential capital is low (2). The apprenticeship converts knowledge into skill. He'll learn soil biology, crop planning, season rhythm, and whether the hands-in-dirt feeling holds up over six months of actual labor.

- Time: 24 hours/week (4 days x 6 hours), March through October.
- Income: $400/month stipend. Net cost after reduced coffee shop hours: approximately $400-600/month of income reduction.
- Startup cost: $0 (no housing needed — Lin lives in Portland). Bike maintenance for Canby commute: $50.
- Capital built: Experiential (primary — hands-on growing skills), Living (soil knowledge, crop understanding), Intellectual (practical agriculture), Social (farm community, mentor relationships).
- Risk: Income compression. Mitigated by afternoon coffee shop shifts and income-based loan repayment.
- QoL check: Done by 1pm. Afternoons free for shop shifts. Evenings and Sundays protected.

### Enterprise 3: The Food Connection Workshop — The Piece That's His
**Role: Multiplier**

A monthly Saturday morning workshop at the community garden: "Grow Your Own." Lin teaches neighborhood residents the basics of growing food — starting seeds, reading soil, building a small bed, seasonal planning. Pete co-teaches. Cost: essentially nothing. The community garden has the space. Lin has the knowledge and the connector instinct.

**Why this enterprise, for this person:** Lin named the gap himself: people care about food systems but can't grow a tomato. He's a natural connector — he created the coffee-grounds-to-garden pipeline without being asked. This workshop is the smallest possible version of filling that gap. It builds a track record, teaches him whether he can hold a group, and creates proof of commitment. When he applies to the PSU Food Systems certificate or any food systems role, this is the line on his resume that says "I didn't just talk about it."

- Time: One Saturday morning per month (3-4 hours including prep).
- Income: $0 initially. Could become donation-based or fee-based ($10-15/person) by month 3-4.
- Startup cost: $0-50 (printed handouts, seed packets for participants).
- Capital built: Social (neighborhood relationships, reputation), Experiential (teaching, facilitation), Intellectual (he'll learn more by teaching), Cultural (bridging food knowledge across communities).
- Synergy with farm apprenticeship: everything Lin learns at the farm feeds directly into workshop content.
- QoL check: One Saturday per month. Joy-generating. Community-building.

### Enterprise 4: Body Practice — Protect the Infrastructure
**Role: Foundation**

Not something to build. Something to maintain. Lin's body is his strongest dimension: biking everywhere, cooking every night, physical energy, time outdoors. When schedules get chaotic and money gets tight — and they will during the apprenticeship months — the body practice is the thing that cannot be cut.

- The bike commute stays (even to Canby — 30 minutes by car, longer by bike, but Lin has done worse).
- Cooking at home stays (cheaper than eating out, better food, joy-generating).
- Sunday rest stays (non-negotiable protected day).
- Community garden time stays (the 4x8 bed is his meditation).

- Time: Embedded in daily life. Not a separate line item.
- Cost: Bike maintenance ($30-50/month), groceries ($250-300/month cooking at home).
- Capital built: Living (health), Spiritual (grounding), Joy (body as pleasure, not obligation).
- QoL check: Did you bike, cook, and rest this week? Y/N.

## Nodal Interventions: The First Two Moves

### Move 1: Complete the Farm Internship Application (This Saturday)

The application was started and not finished. Finishing it is the follow-through test — not the internship itself, but the act of completing what was started.

**Cascade:**
- Completing the application (Experiential capital: proof to himself that he finishes things)
- Sends his name to the farm (Social capital: real person in their system)
- Creates a deadline that organizes everything else (schedule, budget, spring planning)
- Forces the income-based repayment call to the loan servicer
- Shifts identity from "exploring" to "applying"

Time required: 2-3 hours. One Saturday morning.

### Move 2: Call Dani at the People's Food Co-op (Monday)

Not email. A phone call. Fifteen minutes. Tell her he's interested. Ask what's available.

**Cascade:**
- Opens a relationship inside the community food system (Social capital)
- Creates a potential income source or bridge for after the internship (Financial capital)
- Puts Lin inside the co-op infrastructure he's been admiring from outside (Intellectual capital)
- Connects him to the broader Portland food network through Dani's relationships

Time required: 15 minutes.

## The Insight

Lin doesn't have a direction problem. He has a commitment problem wearing a direction costume. He knows it's food systems. He's known since he stayed three hours past his time at that farm and forgot his phone existed. Every application, every new idea, every "or maybe" is a way of staying in the exciting pre-commitment phase where everything is possible and nothing is hard.

The antidote isn't more options. It's ninety days of one thing.

The farm internship is not the answer to "what should I do with my life." It's the answer to "what should I do next." And doing the next thing is the only way the life answer ever arrives.

His parents built something real with food. He's building something real with food. The shape is different, the material is the same. When he's two months into the internship with soil under his fingernails and a workshop under his belt, the conversation with his mom changes from defending a theory to describing a reality. That's when the cultural capital his family carries — twenty years of feeding people, of running a food business, of knowing what it takes — becomes available to him instead of opposed to him.

Commitment to one thing doesn't close doors. It opens the door he's standing in front of.
