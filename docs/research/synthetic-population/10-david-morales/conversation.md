## Phase 1: Ikigai (5 exchanges)

**HUMA:** What's the thing you keep coming back to — the idea that won't leave you alone, even when you're doing something else?

**David:** I bought a lot. Commercial lot in East Austin, about six thousand square feet. I want to build a food stall with a community kitchen attached. That's the idea. It won't shut up.

**HUMA:** You bought it already. Not "thinking about buying." You bought it.

**David:** Yeah. Closed in November. I spent twelve years as a line cook and sous chef, burned out, started a food truck two years ago. The truck does fine — corporate lunches, events, catering gigs. But it's a grind, and I'm alone in a commissary kitchen fourteen hours a day. The lot is the next thing. I just don't know how to get from here to there.

**HUMA:** What are you cooking?

**David:** Oaxacan food. My parents are from Oaxaca — my mom still makes mole negro from scratch, three days of work. I grew up in that kitchen. On the truck I do a simplified version — tlayudas, memelas, a rotating mole. People lose their minds over it. Austin has a million taquerias but almost nobody doing real Oaxacan food. That's not arrogance, that's just the market.

**HUMA:** It's not arrogance. You're describing a gap you were born to fill. The food connects to your family, the skills connect to twelve years of professional cooking, and the market confirms it. That's rare. Most people have one of those three. You have all of them.

**David:** I've never put it that way. It's just what I do.

## Phase 3: Situation Reading — Hybrid (8 exchanges)

**HUMA:** Let's look at what you actually have, starting with the lot. Tell me about the physical space — not what you want to build on it, just what's there right now.

**David:** Six thousand square feet, corner lot on a side street off East Cesar Chavez. Zoned CS — commercial services. Currently empty, crushed gravel pad, chain link fence. Previous owner had a used car lot. There's a concrete slab on the back third, maybe eight hundred square feet, that was some kind of office or shop. Electrical is run to the property, water and sewer are at the street. It's flat, it drains fine, no trees.

**HUMA:** Corner lot on a side street off Cesar Chavez. That's foot traffic territory. What's the neighborhood like around it?

**David:** East Austin is changing fast. Five years ago it was all auto shops and tiendas. Now there's breweries, coffee shops, new construction everywhere. The block has a barbershop, a tire shop that's probably going to sell soon, and a new apartment building going up across the street. Lot of foot traffic, especially evenings and weekends. The food truck parks in the area do well — there's one two blocks away that's packed every night. My lot has better visibility than most of them because of the corner.

**HUMA:** What's the permit situation? Austin has opinions about food service construction.

**David:** I've been through the city website. I need a site plan, a building permit, health department plan review, a food establishment permit, and probably a conditional use permit for the community kitchen piece. The commercial kitchen requires a grease trap, a three-compartment sink, hood ventilation, fire suppression. I've talked to one contractor who does restaurant buildouts — he quoted ninety to a hundred and ten thousand for the food stall side. The community kitchen would be another forty to sixty on top of that if I do it at the same time. That's more than I have.

**HUMA:** So the stall is affordable but the community kitchen pushes you past your capital. That's useful to know — it means they might need to happen in sequence, not simultaneously. Let's set the lot aside for a moment. Tell me about your money situation beyond the savings.

**David:** The truck grosses about a hundred and twenty thousand a year. After food costs, commissary rent, gas, insurance, maintenance, I net around eighty. I've been disciplined about saving — lived cheap since the restaurant days because kitchen pay was garbage. The hundred twenty in savings is real. No debt except a truck payment, fourteen months left on that. Credit's fine. I could probably get an SBA loan but I don't love the idea of debt on a new business.

**HUMA:** Eighty net on a food truck with no employees is strong. The discipline shows. Now — the part that's harder to talk about. You said you don't have a partner, don't have a team, and don't ask for help. What does your life look like outside the truck?

**David:** ...it's mostly the truck. I wake up at five, I'm at the commissary by six prepping. I serve lunch from eleven to two, sometimes an afternoon corporate thing. I'm done cleaning by four or five. I go home. I eat. I watch TV or look at construction plans for the lot. I don't really go out. **HUMA:** When's the last time you cooked for someone just because you wanted to? Not for money. Not at an event. Just because.

**David:** My mom's birthday. I made barbacoa and mole coloradito. That was... four months ago. Maybe five.

**HUMA:** You lit up when you said that. The cooking you love is communal. The cooking you do for a living is solitary. That gap is the design problem.

**David:** I know. I just don't know how to fix it with a business plan.

**HUMA:** Your body — twelve years on the line plus two on the truck. How are you holding up physically?

**David:** Fine, actually. I'm strong. Kitchen work keeps you moving. I run three mornings a week, have for years. My knees are a little beat up from standing on concrete but nothing serious. I'm thirty-eight, not fifty-eight. The body's not the problem.

## Phase 4: Enterprise Map (4 exchanges)

**HUMA:** Five things fit here, and they need to come in the right order. The food stall is the anchor — that's obvious. But the community kitchen is actually the more important enterprise, because it solves the problem you can't spreadsheet your way out of.

**David:** What do you mean?

**HUMA:** The community kitchen isn't a kitchen. It's a social system. You run a pop-up night where another cook uses your space. You teach a mole class. A neighborhood kid learns prep work on your line. Every one of those interactions builds the thing you're weakest at — relationships that aren't transactional. The food stall makes money. The community kitchen makes a life. And here's the thing about East Austin right now: the neighborhood is desperate for spaces that aren't another craft cocktail bar. A community kitchen with a Oaxacan food stall attached is a gathering point. That's worth more than the revenue it generates.

**David:** I've thought about that. I just keep getting stuck on the numbers. The stall I can fund. The community kitchen I can't, at least not at the same time.

**HUMA:** Right. So here's the sequence. Enterprise one: the food stall. You build it, you open it, you transfer your truck customers and your catering relationships to the permanent location. That's your revenue base. Enterprise two: catering. You keep your existing catering business running through the transition and after — it's cash flow that doesn't depend on foot traffic. Enterprise three: value-added products. Your mom's mole, dried chilies, salsas — packaged under your brand, sold at the stall counter and at farmers markets. Texas cottage food law lets you sell direct up to fifty thousand. That's a revenue line that scales without labor hours. Enterprise four: the community kitchen. You don't build it as a full commercial space on day one. You build the stall with a slightly larger kitchen than you need, and you offer it for events and classes one or two nights a week. A monthly supper club. A mole class. A popup for a cook you respect. That's the community kitchen program — it starts as a schedule, not a building. Enterprise five, and this is the one you'll resist: you join a food entrepreneurs network or find a business mentor. Someone who's done a buildout before. That's not an enterprise that makes money. It's an enterprise that builds People capital.

**David:** I don't need a mentor. I need a general contractor and a permit expediter.

**HUMA:** You need someone who's been where you are. Not because you're not smart enough to figure it out — you clearly are. But because every decision in a buildout is a decision you've never made before, and making all of them alone is how you've gotten through life so far, and you've told me twice in this conversation that it's the thing that isn't working.

**David:** ...fair.

## Phase 5: Nodal Interventions (3 exchanges)

**HUMA:** Before you pull a permit, before you hire a contractor, before you spend a dollar on construction — host a popup dinner on the lot. This month. Tables on the gravel, string lights, your food cooked on a portable setup. Fifty people. Charge forty dollars a plate. That's two thousand dollars in revenue, but that's not why you do it. You do it because it answers three questions at once: do people want to eat your food in this location? Who shows up? And can you cook for a crowd without being alone?

**David:** A dinner on the lot. Before it's built.

**HUMA:** Before it's built. The gravel is your floor. The chain link is your fence. You do the invite through your truck's social media and your catering contacts. You cook a proper Oaxacan meal — not the truck menu, the real thing. The tlayuda, the mole negro, the mezcal. Your mom's food, your way. The people who come to that dinner are your first community. Some of them will become regulars. Some of them will know a contractor, a designer, a permit person. One of them might be the partner or advisor you don't know you need yet. The lot doesn't need to be finished to start being a gathering place. It just needs you, your food, and an invitation.

**David:** I know how to throw a dinner. I just never thought of doing it as... a business move.

**HUMA:** It's not a business move. It's the first act of the thing you're building. The second intervention: find one person who's done a food buildout in Austin. Not a consultant — a person. Someone who opened a restaurant or a food hall and will sit with you for an hour over coffee. Ask them what they wish they'd known. The Austin food community is tight. You're in it through the truck, but you haven't plugged into the operator side of it. One conversation changes that. The third intervention: after the popup dinner, run a monthly event on the lot. A supper club. Different theme each time. Oaxacan regions, seasonal, collaborative — bring another cook in for one of them. Keep it to thirty people. You're building the community kitchen before you've built the community kitchen. By the time you pull permits, you'll have a mailing list, a network, and a track record at that location.

## Phase 6: Operational Design (3 exchanges)

**HUMA:** Here's what your week looks like during the buildout phase — the next six to nine months before the stall opens. Monday through Wednesday: truck operations. Your bread and butter. Corporate lunches, prep, service. Thursday: construction day. You're on site, meeting the contractor, making decisions. Friday: truck and catering. Saturday: one supper club or popup event per month; otherwise, rest or product development. Sunday: off. Completely off. No prep, no planning, no spreadsheets. You've been working seven days a week for two years. The system needs a day where it stops.

**David:** Sunday off. I can try that.

**HUMA:** After the stall opens, the rhythm shifts. Monday: closed. That's your new Sunday. Tuesday through Saturday: stall service, lunch and dinner. But here's the design constraint that matters — you said "done by seven." So the stall closes at seven. Not eight, not nine, not "whenever the last customer leaves." Seven. The kitchen is designed for that: a focused menu, fast execution, high throughput. You're not running a full-service restaurant. You're running a food stall with a counter and a patio. That means smaller menu, no table service, faster turns. Your revenue per hour is higher and your labor per hour is lower. You close at seven, you clean until eight, you're home by eight-thirty. That protects the evening.

**David:** And the community kitchen nights?

**HUMA:** One evening a week — Wednesday or Thursday, when the stall is slower or closed. One class or one popup. That's it for the first six months. You'll want to do more. Don't. Build the muscle of running events with other people before you scale it. The validation check is simple: at the end of each week, ask yourself two questions. Did I eat dinner away from the kitchen at least three nights? And did I have a conversation this week that wasn't about food or business? If both answers are yes, the system is working. If either is no, something needs to change — and it's a design problem, not a discipline problem.

**David:** Three dinners away from the kitchen. A conversation that's not about food. That's a low bar.

**HUMA:** It's your bar, David. You set it when you told me the commissary is eating your life. Start there. The bar moves when the system moves.
