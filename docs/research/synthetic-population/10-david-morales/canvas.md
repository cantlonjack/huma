**David Morales** · East Austin, Texas · 6,000 sq ft commercial lot

*"The food connects to everything. The problem is I've been the only one at the table."*

---

## Your Essence

David is a chef whose cooking carries two lineages — twelve years in professional kitchens and a lifetime absorbing the Oaxacan food tradition through his mother's hands. He burned out of restaurant work not because he stopped loving cooking but because the kitchens he worked in reduced food to production. He pivoted to a food truck two years ago and built it into an eighty-thousand-dollar-a-year solo operation. The food is exceptional. Austin is hungry for real Oaxacan cooking. But the truck confirmed a pattern — doing everything himself, in silence, until work is the whole of his life.

What brings him alive is cooking that is visible and communal. An open kitchen where people watch the tortillas come off the comal. A table where people sit together. He bought a commercial lot in East Austin because some part of him understood that the business problem and the loneliness problem are the same problem. The food stall is the visible enterprise. The community kitchen is the real one.

---

## Your Holistic Context

**Quality of Life:** Done cooking by seven. Dinner eaten somewhere that is not the kitchen. An open kitchen where people watch. One conversation per week that has nothing to do with food or business. A business that brings people into his life instead of keeping them out.

**Forms of Production:** High-quality, culturally specific food that commands premium prices through authenticity, not volume. Products that extend family traditions into preserved forms. Community events that transform cooking from solitary act to social one.

**Future Resource Base:** In three years, the stall clearing $150K gross with at least one employee. Community kitchen self-sustaining. Value-added products in retail. A network of food-world colleagues deep enough that David is no longer building alone.

## Your Situation

David's situation is hybrid — a physical commercial space and a life-design challenge that cannot be solved by construction alone.

**The Lot:** Six thousand square feet, corner lot on a side street off East Cesar Chavez. Zoned CS — commercial services. Empty gravel pad, chain link fence, 800 sq ft concrete slab on rear third from a former office. Electrical runs to the property, water and sewer connect at the street. Flat, good drainage, no trees.

The location is strong. East Cesar Chavez is one of Austin's most active food corridors, and the corner visibility catches evening and weekend foot traffic naturally. A food truck park two blocks away draws crowds nightly — market validation at this exact location. The neighborhood is gentrifying rapidly: breweries, coffee shops, new apartment construction replacing auto shops and vacant lots. The demographic shift brings higher spending power but also tension — East Austin's Mexican and Mexican-American community is being priced out of the neighborhood David's food is rooted in. A Oaxacan food stall run by a son of Oaxacan immigrants on this block is a cultural anchor as much as a business.

**Construction:** Austin's permitting process for food service is substantial — site plan, building permit, health department plan review, food establishment permit, and likely a conditional use permit for community kitchen programming. Commercial kitchen requires grease trap, three-compartment sink, hood ventilation, fire suppression. Stall buildout quoted at $90-110K. Community kitchen adds $40-60K — exceeding capital. The design decision: build the stall with a slightly oversized kitchen that can accommodate community events, defer full community kitchen build to Year 2.

**Financial (4/5):** $120K savings, lot paid for, truck netting $80K/year. Debt-free in 14 months. Can fund stall from savings if truck income covers living expenses during construction.

**People (2/5 — Critical):** No partner, no team, no close Austin friendships. Social contact consists of serving food through a window and ordering from vendors. Pattern of doing everything alone until hitting a wall. The buildout risks deepening this if approached the same way.

**Body (4/5):** Strong. Runs 3x/week. Not a limiting factor.

**Joy (3/5):** Deep joy in communal cooking. The truck has been draining it by making cooking solitary. The last time his cooking felt right was making mole coloradito for his mother's birthday.

## Your Nodal Interventions

### 1. Host a Popup Dinner on the Empty Lot

This month. Before permits, before construction. Fifty seats, $40/plate, portable cooking setup, tables on gravel, string lights. Full Oaxacan menu — not the truck menu, the real thing. The dinner answers three questions: does the location work? Who is the community? Can you cook surrounded by people instead of alone? The mailing list from this dinner seeds everything that follows. Investment: ~$1,500. Revenue: ~$2,000.

### 2. Find One Food Buildout Mentor

Within two weeks. One coffee meeting. Contact Comida Austin, AIBA, or a direct ask through truck contacts. Find one person who's completed a food service buildout in Austin. One conversation saves you from ten-thousand-dollar mistakes. More importantly, it breaks the pattern — David asks for help, the world does not end, he does it again.

### 3. Launch a Monthly Supper Club

Month 2, after the popup. Thirty seats, different Oaxacan theme each month. One month, invite another cook to collaborate. Each dinner adds to the mailing list, deepens relationships, markets the stall before it opens. By month six, David opens the stall to an audience that already knows him, the food, and the space. The community kitchen is built before the community kitchen is built. Investment: ~$800/event. Revenue: ~$1,200.

## Validation

**Weekly (2 minutes):** Did I eat dinner away from the kitchen 3+ nights? Did I have one non-food conversation? Is revenue on track?

**Quarterly:** Revenue by enterprise vs. plan. Social capital — how many real relationships this quarter? Am I closing at seven, or creeping past it? Does the cooking still feel like mine?

When a metric is off, look at the system. If evenings disappear, it is the menu being too complex or the lack of help at the counter. Design problems, not discipline problems.
