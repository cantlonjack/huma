## Phase 1: Ikigai

**HUMA:** Tell me about where you are and what you do.

**James:** I run a grass-fed beef operation and farm store in Addison County. Third generation on this land. About 280 acres, 180 head. We transitioned off dairy fifteen years ago.

**HUMA:** That's a real shift — dairy to beef on a place that's been milking for decades. What made you pull that trigger?

**James:** Milk price. It was killing us. I looked at the numbers and they weren't going to get better. So I sold the bulk tank, sold most of the Holsteins, bought some Devons and started finishing on grass. My father thought I'd lost my mind.

**HUMA:** And now?

**James:** The farm's fine. The store's fine. I'm not here because something's broken. I'm here because my wife died two years ago and I'm sixty-two and I need to figure out what I'm doing this for if it's just me now.

**HUMA:** Yeah. That changes the question, doesn't it. Not "what should you build" but "what is this for."

**James:** That's about right. Margaret handled the store, the community side, the farmers' market. She was the reason people came back. I'm the one who moves the cattle and fixes the fence. Without her the whole thing tilts.

**HUMA:** What do you love about the work itself — not the outcome, the doing?

**James:** Moving cattle in the morning when the grass is wet. Reading the pastures. Knowing this piece needs rest, that piece is ready. The land's in better shape than it's been in my lifetime. That means something. I just don't know what it means if it's only me seeing it.

## Phase 2: Holistic Context

**HUMA:** Let's talk about what a good life actually looks like for you right now — not what it used to look like with Margaret, but what it needs to be going forward. What matters most in a given week?

**James:** I want to keep doing the work. I'm not retiring. The body's slower but it still works. I want the store to keep going because people depend on it — we're one of three places in the county you can get local beef year-round. And I guess I need to figure out the people piece. I've gotten pretty isolated. Margaret handled that. I just showed up when she told me to.

**HUMA:** That isolation — is it the farmhouse at night, or is it all day?

**James:** Nights are hard. Days are fine. I've got work to do all day. But I come in at six and it's just quiet. Used to be she'd have the store numbers, or some neighbor stopped by, or she'd be planning the next market. Now I eat and watch the weather and go to bed. Weekends are worse. She made me go to things.

**HUMA:** So the farm day has a rhythm that holds you. But the life around the farm day has a hole in it. What would it look like if that part worked — not the way it was, but some version that fit who you are without Margaret designing it for you?

**James:** I don't know. I'm not a social person. Never was. But I need some reason to talk to people that isn't just selling them beef. Margaret had that. I don't have it naturally. But I notice when it's been four days and I haven't talked to anyone but the dog, and that's not right either.

**HUMA:** That's honest. And it's a design problem, not a personality problem. Your QoL needs a social layer that fits a man who's not going to join a book club. We'll come back to what that looks like when we get to your enterprises — because the answer is probably already on the farm. What about the money side? Does the operation cover what you need?

**James:** More than covers it. The house is paid for. Equipment's in good shape. The beef sells itself — we've got a waiting list for halves and quarters. Store does $120,000 a year, beef another $80,000 direct. I'm not rich, but I don't worry about money.

## Phase 3: Situation Reading (Regrarians)

**HUMA:** Let's read your place. I want to go layer by layer — the permanent stuff first, then down to the flexible. Start with climate. What does Addison County give you and what does it take?

**James:** Zone 4b. We get real winter — minus twenty some nights in January. Growing season's about 140 days, but the grass season is longer. Good snow cover most years, which helps the pastures. Spring mud is brutal. We get enough rain, usually — 36 to 40 inches. Last couple years have been drier in August than they used to be.

**HUMA:** Water on the property?

**James:** Otter Creek runs through the south end. Two spring-fed ponds, one on the east hill and one near the barns. I ran waterlines to the paddocks twelve years ago — frost-free hydrants every four paddocks. That was one of the best investments I ever made. We're not water-limited.

**HUMA:** Good infrastructure decision. Access — roads, markets, neighbors?

**James:** County road frontage, quarter mile to Route 7. Middlebury's ten minutes. Burlington's forty-five. The store sits right on the road — Margaret picked that spot. We're close enough to the college and the resort traffic that we get drive-by customers June through October. Good location.

**HUMA:** Walk me through the buildings.

**James:** Main barn — the old dairy barn. We converted it when we switched. It's handling equipment, hay storage. Machine shed. The store is a converted sugarhouse, 800 square feet, walk-in cooler Margaret made me put in. Farmhouse — 1890s, good bones, too big for one person. There's a small apartment above the store that Margaret was going to rent out but never got to.

**HUMA:** That apartment — is it finished?

**James:** Roughed in. Plumbing and electric are there. Needs drywall, flooring, a kitchen. Maybe $15,000 to finish. Margaret had plans for it. I just never got around to it.

**HUMA:** Soils?

**James:** Champlain Valley clay loams mostly. Heavy when wet, productive when managed. The hilltop pastures are thinner — Vergennes clay up there. We've been building organic matter for fifteen years with the rotation. Soil tests are the best they've been. The biology is coming back. You can smell it in spring.

**HUMA:** How are you managing the rotation right now?

**James:** Thirty-two paddocks. I move them every one to three days depending on the grass. Some of the bigger paddocks I'd like to split but the fencing is a project. Rest periods of 60 to 90 days in the growing season. I stockpile for fall grazing — that saves me about six weeks of hay. I've got 60 acres in hay for winter, and I buy another 200 round bales from a neighbor.

**HUMA:** Your operation is already penciling out. You're not here to add revenue. **James:** We're getting about $6.50 a pound hanging weight on halves and quarters. Wholes go for $5.75. Store cuts are marked up to $8-14 depending on the cut. We process through a USDA plant in Rutland. I'd say we're capturing most of the value. The waiting list tells me we could charge more, but I don't want to price out the families who've been buying from us for ten years.

**HUMA:** That's a choice that matters — and it tells me the store is doing more than selling beef. It's holding a community relationship Margaret built. Here's what I'd put in front of you. One: finish that apartment above the store and use it for a working apprentice or intern position.
