# Operator 07 — Thomas Hawkins
## Full Design Mode Conversation

**Profile:** 55, Blue Ridge Mountains, western North Carolina. Vietnam-era veteran's son, former contractor, 18 months sober. Forty acres of homesteaded family land with timber, spring-fed creek, cabin under repair. Archetype: recovering. Communication: guarded. Follow-through: 0.4.

**Dimensions:** identity=2, purpose=2, body=2, home=3, growth=2, money=2, people=3, joy=2

---

## Phase 1 — Ikigai (6 exchanges)

**HUMA:**
Hey Thomas. Before we get into the land and all the rest of it — what's the thing that's been sitting in the back of your mind lately? The one you keep coming back to, even when you're doing something else.

**Thomas:**
Honestly? I keep thinking about what I'm supposed to be doing with all this time. I used to run crews. Sixteen guys, commercial framing. Now it's just me and forty acres and a lot of quiet. I'm not complaining about the quiet. I'm just not sure what to do with it.

**HUMA:**
Sixteen guys is a lot of people to be responsible for. That kind of structure — someone else setting the schedule, the crew depending on you to show up — that held things together for a long time. What does a day look like now, without it?

**Thomas:**
I get up. Coffee. Work on the cabin — there's always something. Right now it's the porch joists, they're shot. I work until I run out of daylight or run out of knowing what to do next. Some days I drive into town for materials. Some days I just walk the property. That's about it.

**HUMA:**
The cabin work — is that something you're doing because it needs doing, or because it's the one thing that still makes sense?

**Thomas:**
Both, I guess. It does need doing. But yeah. When I'm cutting a joist or fitting a board, I know what I'm doing. I know what comes next. It's the rest of the day that's the problem. I'm not the kind of guy who sits around well.

**HUMA:**
You said you know how to work — you just don't know what you're working toward. That's not a small thing to say. When the crew was going, when the business was running, what was the thing underneath the work that made it worth getting up for?

**Thomas:**
I don't know if I thought about it that way. I was good at it. I could look at a set of plans and see the building. I could take a pile of lumber and make something that would stand for fifty years. I liked that. Making something real. Something you could put your hand on. I guess that's still in there somewhere.

**HUMA:**
Making something real that lasts. Something you can put your hand on. That's not a small thing — that's an identity. And the land your grandparents homesteaded is the one thing that's still standing from before. What does that property mean to you, if you set aside the practical stuff for a minute?

**Thomas:**
It's the only thing the drinking didn't take. I lost the business, the marriage, the license for a year. But Pawpaw's place is still here. The spring still runs. The tulip poplars he planted along the drive are sixty feet tall now. I don't know. It's mine. It's the one thing I didn't wreck.

[[CONTEXT: Thomas's essence is rooted in making — building things that last with his hands. The land is not just property; it's the surviving proof that something endured. His identity is bound to craftsmanship and to this specific place. The quiet is both healing and threatening — without structure, the days blur. He needs a reason to get up that's built into the land itself.]]

## Phase 2 — Holistic Context (5 exchanges)

**HUMA:**
Those tulip poplars your grandfather planted are doing exactly what they were designed to do sixty years later. That's the kind of building you understand. Let me ask you something different — when you think about what you want your days to actually feel like, not five years from now, just this coming season, what matters most?

**Thomas:**
I want to be tired at the end of the day for the right reasons. Not just because I stayed busy. I want to know that what I did today connects to something tomorrow. And I want to be able to look at what I built at the end of a month and see it adding up to something.

**HUMA:**
Tired for the right reasons. That's a clear signal. Let me push on the money side for a minute, because it shapes what's possible. Where are things at financially? Not looking for details — just the rough shape.

**Thomas:**
I get a small disability check from the VA — not combat, a back injury from the job. That covers groceries and the electric bill. I've got no debt because I've got nothing to borrow against except the land, and I won't do that. But there's no cushion. If the truck breaks down, that's a problem. I pick up cash work when I can — deck repairs, odd jobs for neighbors. Maybe five, six hundred a month when it's steady. It's not enough to build anything with.

**HUMA:**
So the floor is covered but there's no room to move. The cash work keeps the lights on but it's reactive — you're waiting for someone else to need something. What would it look like if the land itself started producing income, even small amounts?

**Thomas:**
That's what I keep circling around. I've got forty acres of hardwood timber, a creek, a good spring, southern exposure on the lower field. I know there's something there. I just don't know how to think about it as a business. Running a framing crew is different from running a farm. I know wood. I know tools. I don't know markets or pricing or any of that.

**HUMA:**
You know more than you think. You know timber — species, grain, what's worth cutting and what's worth leaving. You know building. You know how to manage a crew, which means you know how to manage a project. The gap isn't knowledge. It's structure. You need a weekly rhythm that connects the land to income, and that connects both of those to the feeling you described — tired for the right reasons, with something adding up. What would need to be true for you to feel like this place is working, not just standing?

**Thomas:**
I'd need to see money coming in from the land itself. Not a lot. But steady. Something I could count on every month. And I'd need to know what I'm doing each morning when I wake up. That's the part that gets me. The not-knowing. On a job site, the foreman hands you the schedule. Here there's nobody handing me anything.

[[CONTEXT: Thomas's holistic context centers on structure, daily purpose, and modest but steady income from the land. His VA disability and cash work cover survival but not stability. He has zero debt and refuses to leverage the land. His capital base is physical skill (carpentry, forestry, building) and forty acres of productive Appalachian land. The critical need is not ambition but rhythm — a weekly structure that replaces the job site schedule. Financial target is modest: $800-1200/month from land-based enterprise would transform his situation. Key constraint: he doesn't think in terms of markets or business, only in terms of making things. The bridge is enterprises that convert making into income.]]

## Phase 3 — Situation Reading / Regrarians (8 exchanges)

**HUMA:**
All right. Let's walk your forty acres together, layer by layer. Start with the big picture — what's your climate like up there in the Blue Ridge? Elevation, rainfall, how long is your growing season?

**Thomas:**
I'm at about 2,800 feet. Zone 6b, maybe 7a down by the creek — it's warmer in the bottomland. We get around 50 inches of rain, spread pretty even through the year. Last frost is usually mid-April, first frost mid-October. So maybe five and a half months of real growing season. Winters aren't brutal but they're real. We get snow, it sticks for a week or two at a time. January and February are cold. Fog in the mornings a lot of the year.

**HUMA:**
Good rainfall, decent growing season for that elevation. The temperature differential between the ridge and the creek bottom is worth paying attention to — that's two different growing environments on the same property. How does the land lay? What's the terrain?

**Thomas:**
It runs from the ridge down to the creek. Maybe 400 feet of elevation change over the whole property. The cabin sits about midway, on a bench — flat spot, maybe two acres, where my grandparents cleared for the house and the old garden. Above the cabin it's steep, mostly north-facing, heavy timber. Below the cabin it slopes south-southwest down to the creek bottom. That lower slope is more open — some old pasture that's growing up in blackberry and sassafras. The creek bottom is flat, maybe three acres, but it floods in spring.

**HUMA:**
That south-southwest slope below the cabin — that's your best ground. Southern exposure at that elevation, with the creek at the bottom for water access. How wide is the creek? Does it run year-round?

**Thomas:**
Year-round. It's maybe eight feet wide in summer, knee-deep in the pools. Spring it gets up to fifteen feet and covers the bottom. There's a spring higher up on the north side that feeds into it — that's where my water comes from, gravity-fed to the cabin. Good water. Cold. Never goes dry, even in August.

**HUMA:**
Gravity-fed spring water that never fails is worth more than most people realize. That's your most permanent asset after the land itself. What about access — how do you get in and out, and how far are you from town?

**Thomas:**
Gravel road, county-maintained, comes in from the highway. It's about twelve miles to the nearest town — that's Marshall, the county seat. Asheville is forty minutes. I've got a driveway that runs from the road up to the cabin, and an old logging road that goes up the ridge from behind the cabin. It's rough but passable with the truck in dry weather.

**HUMA:**
Twelve miles to Marshall, forty minutes to Asheville. That Asheville market is important — it's one of the best small-city markets in the southeast for local products. People drive further than forty minutes to sell there. Tell me about your timber. You said hardwood — what species are you working with?

**Thomas:**
Mostly oak — white oak, red oak, some chestnut oak up on the ridge. Good stand of tulip poplar, especially along the drive and the creek. Some hickory, black walnut scattered through. A few big hemlocks down by the creek, though some of them are dying from the woolly adelgid. The north-facing slope above the cabin is the densest — it hasn't been logged since my grandfather did select cuts in the sixties. Some of those oaks are three feet through. Below the cabin, the old pasture has volunteer red cedar, dogwood, serviceberry coming up. A few old apple trees from when my grandmother had an orchard — they still fruit, barely.

**HUMA:**
White oak and black walnut — those are high-value species if you're thinking timber. But the real opportunity might be what's between the trees, not the trees themselves. Those big oaks on the north slope are perfect substrate for shiitake logs. And the old apple trees tell you the soil and the microclimate on that lower slope can grow things. What about buildings — besides the cabin, what's standing?

**Thomas:**
The cabin — that's a real structure, not a shack. My grandfather built it in '52. Chestnut logs for the original part, frame addition in the seventies. I've been rebuilding the porch, replaced the roof two years ago. There's a root cellar dug into the hillside behind it, stone-lined, still sound. An old chicken house that's mostly fallen in. And a log outbuilding — used to be a workshop or maybe a smokehouse — that's got a good roof and stone foundation but needs a new floor and one wall. It's maybe twelve by sixteen.

**HUMA:**
That outbuilding on a stone foundation with a good roof — that's a building waiting to be useful. And the root cellar is climate-controlled storage that most people would pay thousands to build. What do you know about your soils? Have you ever had them tested, or do you just know from what grows?

**Thomas:**
Never tested. I know the bottomland by the creek is dark and deep — my grandmother grew everything down there. The bench where the cabin sits is decent, some clay. Up on the ridge it's thin and rocky. The old pasture slope has good topsoil where it hasn't eroded, but there's some gullying where the water runs off. I'd guess it's acidic — everything up here is. The rhododendrons and mountain laurel are happy, and those like acid.

**HUMA:**
Acidic soils in the Blue Ridge — that's typical, and it matters for what you grow. But acid soils and hardwood shade are exactly what shiitake mushrooms want. Here's what I'm seeing as a whole picture:

You're sitting on forty acres that divides into three distinct zones. The north-facing ridge timber — that's your bank account growing slowly, and your source of mushroom logs and firewood. The south-facing slope below the cabin — that's your production ground, with the old pasture ready to be reclaimed, southern exposure, and creek access at the bottom. And the cabin bench itself — the buildings, the root cellar, the workshop, the flat ground — that's your operations center.

The spring-fed water, the Asheville market within driving distance, and your own hands are the three assets that tie it all together. The land has been waiting for someone to work it again. It's been patient. The question is what kind of work fits you, fits this place, and starts producing without requiring capital you don't have.

[[CONTEXT: Thomas's forty acres in the Blue Ridge (2,800 ft, Zone 6b/7a) divides into three zones: (1) North-facing ridge — mature hardwood timber (white oak, red oak, tulip poplar, hickory, black walnut), unlogged since 1960s select cuts, steep, dense canopy. (2) South-southwest slope — old pasture growing up in pioneer species, southern exposure, dropping 400 ft to creek, some erosion gullying. (3) Creek bottom — 3 acres flat alluvial soil, floods spring, year-round creek 8-15 ft wide. Key infrastructure: gravity-fed spring (never fails), log cabin (1952, under repair), stone root cellar (sound), collapsed chicken house, log outbuilding 12x16 (good roof/foundation, needs floor and wall). Access: 12 mi to Marshall, 40 min to Asheville market. Old logging road to ridge. Soils: deep alluvial at creek, clay bench at cabin, thin/rocky on ridge, acid throughout. Existing trees of note: 60-ft tulip poplars, 3-ft diameter oaks, scattered black walnut, old apple trees still fruiting. Woolly adelgid affecting hemlocks.]]

## Phase 4 — Enterprise Map (4 exchanges)

**HUMA:**
Here's what I'd put in front of you. Not a five-year plan. Four enterprises that fit your hands, your land, and where you are right now — and one of them you can start this weekend.

**Selective Timber & Forestry Management** — Foundation

This is what you already know. You've got mature hardwood on that north slope that hasn't been managed in sixty years. Selective thinning — taking the cull trees, the crowded stems, the storm-damaged ones — improves the stand and produces income. White oak and black walnut are premium species. A single black walnut sawlog can bring $200-500. You're not clear-cutting. You're tending a forest your grandfather started. Year one, selective thinning could bring $3,000-6,000 from a single carefully planned harvest. Beyond sawlogs, the tops and smaller wood become firewood — seasoned hardwood firewood sells for $250-350 a cord near Asheville, and you've got the chainsaw skills and the truck to deliver. Three to five cords a month in winter is $750-1,750 in the months when nothing else is producing. Startup cost: you already own the tools. Time: 10-15 hours a week in season. This enterprise does three things at once — it generates immediate income, it produces the oak logs your mushroom enterprise needs, and it improves the timber stand for the future.

**Shiitake Mushrooms on Oak Logs** — Anchor

Your north slope is a shiitake farm waiting to happen. Dense shade, acid soil, hardwood logs from your own thinning operation, and creek water for soaking. You drill fresh-cut oak logs, inoculate with shiitake spawn, stack them in the shade, and wait. Six to twelve months later, they fruit. A hundred logs is a modest start — that's maybe two weekends of work to cut, drill, and inoculate. By year two, a hundred logs can produce 200-400 pounds of shiitake at $12-16 a pound. That's $2,400-6,400 from logs that cost you nothing but labor and $150 in spawn. Asheville restaurants and the farmers market pay premium for local shiitake. The root cellar is your cold storage. The log outbuilding becomes your inoculation workshop once you rebuild the floor. This enterprise is forgiving. If you miss a week, the logs don't die. They wait. For someone at 0.4 follow-through, that matters more than anything else I could say about it.

**Cabin Carpentry & Custom Woodwork** — Multiplier

You've been rebuilding your own cabin with your own hands. That's not just maintenance — that's a portfolio. There are people within thirty miles of you who own old cabins, barns, and mountain houses that need exactly the kind of work you do. Log repair, timber framing, porch rebuilds, custom shelving from your own milled lumber. This isn't contracting — no crews, no liability for sixteen guys, no commercial bids. This is one skilled carpenter doing the work he's best at, on his own schedule, for $40-65 an hour. Two days a week of outside carpentry work is $2,500-4,000 a month. You set the pace. You pick the jobs. The rest of the week belongs to the land. This also solves the structure problem — Tuesday and Thursday, you're on someone else's site. Monday, Wednesday, Friday, you're on your own land doing forestry and mushroom work. The week has shape.

**Honey Bees** — Long Game

Five hives along the edge of the old pasture, where the blackberry and tulip poplar give them forage from April through August. Startup is $1,500-2,000 for the hives, bees, and basic equipment. You won't harvest the first year — the colonies need to build. Year two, five hives produce 150-250 pounds of honey at $12-15 a pound. That's $1,800-3,750. More importantly, bees give you a daily check-in — a reason to walk the property every morning and observe something alive that depends on you in a gentle way. The bees don't need a foreman. They just need someone paying attention. And the Asheville honey market is strong.

The total picture: forestry and firewood for immediate cash and winter income. Mushrooms for spring-through-fall revenue that builds over time. Carpentry two days a week for steady money and structure. Bees for the long game and the daily walk. Year one, conservative: $12,000-18,000 combined. Year three: $20,000-30,000. Added to your VA check, that's a working life.

**Thomas:**
That's... more than I expected. The timber and the carpentry I can see. Those make sense to me. The mushrooms — I've heard about people doing that but I don't know the first thing about inoculating logs. And the bees — I don't know. That's a lot of new things at once.

**HUMA:**
Fair. Let me be clear — you don't start all four at once. You start with one. The mushrooms: the first thing about inoculating logs is that it's drilling holes and pushing in plugs. It's the simplest part of the whole thing. If you can run a drill press, you can inoculate a log. The technique takes an afternoon to learn. There's a guy named Craig Shelton who runs mushroom workshops at the WNC Farmers Market — that would be your starting point. But we don't have to decide about mushrooms today. What matters is which one you'd actually do first.

**Thomas:**
The timber. That's what I know. I could walk up there this weekend and start marking trees.

**HUMA:**
That's the right answer. Everything else follows from it. The trees you thin produce the logs for mushrooms. The lumber you mill goes into your carpentry work. The forest management makes the property healthier for bees. It all starts with walking the north slope with a can of spray paint and your eye for what stays and what goes.

## Phase 5 — Nodal Interventions (2 exchanges)

**HUMA:**
Here's the cascade. One action this weekend starts a chain that feeds everything else for the next six months.

**The node:** This Saturday, walk the north slope with marking paint. Identify ten cull trees — storm-damaged, crowded, poor form. Mark them. Don't cut them yet. Just mark them. That's two hours of walking your own timber with eyes you already have.

Why ten trees is the lever that moves everything:

Ten marked trees become your first thinning cut. That produces four to six sawlogs and a cord or two of firewood. The sawlogs go to the mill or sell as logs — $500-1,500. The firewood seasons through summer for fall sale. The fresh-cut oak tops — the pieces too small for lumber but too good for firewood — those are your first mushroom logs. Twenty to thirty logs from ten trees. Drill and inoculate them in April, they fruit next spring. Meanwhile, the thinning opens the canopy just enough to improve the stand. And walking the timber with a purpose every Saturday becomes the structure your week is missing.

One action. Ten trees. Everything else grows from there.

**Thomas:**
Ten trees. I can do ten trees. I've got marking paint in the truck already.

## Phase 6 — Operational Design (2 exchanges)

**HUMA:**
Here's what your week looks like, starting now. Not eventually. Now.

**Monday / Wednesday / Friday — Land Days**
Morning: property walk. Check the spring, check the cabin project, check whatever's growing. This is your version of a morning briefing — thirty minutes of walking and looking. Then work. Monday is forestry — cutting, hauling, processing the marked trees. Wednesday is cabin repair and maintenance. Friday is the flexible day — mushroom work when the logs are ready, firewood splitting and stacking, equipment maintenance. Stop by 4pm.

**Tuesday / Thursday — Carpentry Days**
Off-property work. Two days a week of paid carpentry keeps cash flowing and gives you the structure of showing up somewhere with a purpose. Start with the neighbors and word of mouth. As the reputation builds, you pick the jobs you want.

**Saturday Morning — Walk the Timber**
Two hours. Marking paint. This is your planning session disguised as a walk. You're learning your forest while you identify the next trees to take. This is the one non-negotiable.

**Sunday — Rest**
You've earned it. The system only works if it stops.

The bees wait until you've got the forestry rhythm down — probably next spring. The mushroom inoculation happens once you've got fresh logs from the first thinning — probably April. Nothing starts before you're ready for it.

How does that feel as a shape for the week?

**Thomas:**
It feels like a job site. I mean that in a good way. I know where to show up. I know what I'm doing. Monday is forestry, Tuesday is carpentry, and so on. I haven't had that in a while. The Saturday walk — yeah. I already do that, I just don't do it with a purpose. Marking paint changes it from wandering to working. That's a small thing but it's not small.

**HUMA:**
It's not small. The difference between wandering and working is a can of spray paint and a reason. You've got both.

Your canvas is ready. Everything we talked about — the essence, the enterprises, the weekly rhythm, the starting point — it's all in one place now. You can come back to it whenever you need to see the whole picture. The land has been waiting. You know what to do with a Saturday morning.
