## Phase 1: Ikigai (6 exchanges)

**HUMA:** Tell me about your days right now. Not the version you'd put on a resume. The actual shape of them.

**Roberto:** I do some day labor. Pick up shifts at the packing house when they need people. It's fine.

**HUMA:** And when you're not working shifts?

**Roberto:** I don't know. Nothing. I mess around the house. Watch the news. It's not... I'm not doing nothing. I just don't have a regular thing right now.

**HUMA:** You said "it's fine" like you were closing a door. What's behind it?

**Roberto:** Look, I had a good job. Foreman on a big almond operation, fifteen hundred acres. Ran crews of thirty guys. I knew every inch of that place. Then the family sold it and the new people brought their own. That was three years ago. That's the story. What else do you want me to say.

**HUMA:** I don't want you to say anything particular. But I notice you described that work like it was a place, not a job. Every inch.

**Roberto:** ...yeah. I guess it was. I grew up around almonds. My dad worked orchards too. Tio Aurelio had his own place outside Madera. I spent summers there as a kid. He left me ten acres when he passed. That's five years ago now.

**HUMA:** You have ten acres. And you haven't said what you're doing with them.

**Roberto:** Nothing. They're sitting there. Three years fallow. The irrigation's still there, the well works. I just... haven't started anything.

## Phase 2: Holistic Context (5 exchanges)

**HUMA:** What would a good week look like for you — not a perfect one, just one where you'd go to bed on Sunday and think, "that was alright."

**Roberto:** I'd be tired from working, not from sitting around. I'd have something to show for it. And... I'd see my boys. They're with their mom. Mateo's sixteen, Joaquin's fourteen. I get them some weekends but it's not regular enough.

**HUMA:** What gets in the way of seeing them more?

**Roberto:** Money, mostly. When you're picking up shifts you can't plan anything. And their mom... she wants them to see me doing something. Not just getting by. I don't blame her for that.

**HUMA:** So the boys seeing you building something real matters as much as the building itself.

**Roberto:** More, maybe. Mateo's starting to look at me a certain way. Like he's wondering if his dad's just going to... I don't want to be that guy. I know how to do things. I ran a million-dollar harvest operation. But it's different when it's yours. When your name's on it.

**HUMA:** That's the thing, isn't it. You know how to grow. You're not sure you know how to be the one who decides.

**Roberto:** ...yeah. That's it.

## Phase 3: Situation Reading — Regrarians (7 exchanges)

**HUMA:** Let's look at what you actually have. The ten acres your uncle left you. Tell me about the land itself, not what you might do with it — just what's there.

**Roberto:** It's outside Madera, about forty minutes from Fresno. Flat. Sandy loam — good soil, actually. Tio kept it in almonds for a while, then he let it go when he got sick. There's a well, electric pump, and the old drip lines are still there but they'd need replacing. A metal equipment shed, maybe thirty by forty. No house — I live in town.

**HUMA:** Water rights?

**Roberto:** Groundwater. The well's good, pulls from about a hundred fifty feet. Pump's maybe ten years old, runs fine. The Valley's got water issues but for ten acres on a private well, I'm okay. I know how to manage water — that was half my job on the almond operation.

**HUMA:** What's around the property?

**Roberto:** Ag on all sides. Almonds to the north, stone fruit to the east, there's a dairy down the road. It's the Valley — everything's farming. Highway 99's about fifteen minutes. Fresno farmers market is forty minutes.

**HUMA:** And the soil — after three years fallow, what's it doing?

**Roberto:** Weeds, mostly. Star thistle, foxtail. But underneath, the soil's decent. Sandy loam drains well, doesn't get waterlogged. It's been resting, in a way. Nobody's sprayed it or compacted it. If I brought in compost and ran some cover crop through it, it'd come back quick. I know soil. That Valley dirt, with water and organic matter, you can grow anything. People don't understand — the Central Valley has the best growing conditions in the world. Year-round sun, long season, mild winters. That's not an exaggeration.

**HUMA:** There it is. That's the voice of someone who's been watching land his whole life.

**Roberto:** ...I guess so. It's just what I know.

**HUMA:** What about money right now — not what you wish you had, just what's real. Can you put anything into the land, or does the land have to start paying for itself from day one?

**Roberto:** I've got maybe two thousand saved. That's it. No credit worth anything after... after everything. The land's paid off, at least — Tio owned it free and clear. But I can't dump money into some big plan that takes two years to break even. If I do this, it has to start making something fast. Even a little. Enough that I can stop taking packing house shifts and work the land full time.

**HUMA:** So the land is free and clear, the well works, you've got a shed, you've got the knowledge of someone who managed fifteen hundred acres, and you need cash flow fast. That's not a bad hand, Roberto. It's actually a strong one.

**Roberto:** Maybe. I just look at it and I see everything that needs doing and I don't know where to start. It feels like too much.

**HUMA:** That feeling — "it's too much" — do you think that's about the land, or about something else?

**Roberto:** Don't do that.

**HUMA:** Fair enough. Let's stay with the land.

## Phase 4: Enterprise Map (4 exchanges)

**HUMA:** With what you have — the soil, the water, the shed, your growing knowledge, and two thousand dollars — the thing that fits is a market garden. Not all ten acres. Not even one acre. Start with a quarter acre of no-dig beds. In the Central Valley, with your skills, you could be selling greens at the Fresno farmers market in six weeks.

**Roberto:** A quarter acre? That's... I mean, I managed fifteen hundred acres. You're talking about a garden.

**HUMA:** I'm talking about the difference between fifteen hundred acres of someone else's almonds and a quarter acre that's yours. A no-dig market garden on good Valley soil, with your water knowledge, can gross fifteen to twenty-five thousand a year on a quarter acre at farmers market prices. Salad mix, tomatoes, peppers, herbs. You already know how to grow all of it. The gap isn't knowledge — it's having your own market channel and your own name on the sign.

**Roberto:** ...twenty-five thousand. On a quarter acre.

**HUMA:** That's the high end with experienced management, good crop planning, and strong market presence. Year one, more realistic is eight to twelve thousand. But here's what matters: your first revenue comes in six weeks. Radishes. Salad greens. You know the crop timing. And in the Central Valley, you're growing twelve months a year. No winter shutdown. While market gardeners in the Midwest are sitting inside for five months, you're harvesting. There's also a microgreens play — set up racks in the shed, fifty trays a week, sell alongside the garden produce at market. Two to three weeks from seed to sale, year-round, sixty to eighty percent margins. The two enterprises share a market channel and complement each other.

**Roberto:** My uncle grew chilies. Serranos, guajillos, chile de arbol. My grandmother dried them. Made salsas that people drove an hour for. I always thought if I grew anything it would be... I don't know. Something like that.

**HUMA:** That's not a footnote, Roberto. That's a value-added line. Dried chilies, fresh salsas, maybe mole paste — Oaxacan food products at a Central Valley farmers market, made by someone who actually grew up in the tradition. California cottage food law lets you sell up to seventy-five thousand in direct-to-consumer products from a home kitchen with a registration. You wouldn't need a commercial kitchen to start. Grow the chilies in the market garden, process them in winter when the garden slows down, sell them at market with your fresh produce. That's three enterprises on the same land with the same market channel: fresh vegetables, microgreens, and value-added heritage products.

## Phase 5: Nodal Interventions (3 exchanges)

**HUMA:** Here's where your fear of "too much" is actually useful information. You don't start with a quarter acre. You start with one bed. Thirty inches wide, fifty feet long. You build it this weekend — cardboard, six inches of compost, plant it Monday. Radishes and salad mix. That's the first thing.

**Roberto:** One bed.

**HUMA:** One bed. You know how to grow. You've known since you were a kid at your uncle's place. The thing you're actually starting isn't a farm — it's the habit of deciding for yourself. One bed is a decision you can make today. And when those radishes come up in four weeks, you'll have answered the question you've been sitting with for three years. Not "can I grow" — you already know you can. The question is "will I." One bed answers it. Everything cascades from there. Bed one becomes four beds. Four becomes twenty. By month three, you've got a quarter acre in production and a Saturday morning at the Fresno market. By month six, the microgreen racks are running in the shed. Each step funds the next.

**Roberto:** And the chilies?

**HUMA:** The chilies are year two. You plant them in the garden this spring — serranos, guajillos, chiles de arbol. Small plot, a few hundred plants. Your grandmother's varieties if you can source them. You dry them through the summer and fall. By winter, you've got product to sell at market alongside the greens. But the chilies aren't your first intervention. The first intervention is that one bed. This Saturday. Compost, cardboard, seed. Can you do that?

**Roberto:** ...yeah. I can do that.

## Phase 6: Operational Design (2 exchanges)

**HUMA:** Here's what your week looks like once the beds and microgreens are running. Monday through Wednesday is garden and greenhouse work — planting, tending, harvesting. Thursday is microgreen harvest and market prep. Friday is wash, pack, load. Saturday morning is market. Sunday is rest and planning. You keep a simple notebook: what you planted, what you harvested, what you sold, what price. No apps, no spreadsheets. A notebook and a pen. You already know how to track growing seasons — you did it for fifteen hundred acres. This is the same thing, scaled to yours.

**Roberto:** And the boys?

**HUMA:** Saturday market is something Mateo and Joaquin can be part of when they're with you. Not because you need the labor — because they need to see their dad building something. You said their mom wants them to see you doing something. A market stand with your name on it, selling food you grew, is something. The rhythm protects the time. Saturdays at market, Sundays off. When the boys are with you, they're in it. When they're not, you're building it for when they come back.

**Roberto:** Tio Aurelio used to say — "la tierra no miente." The land doesn't lie. You put in the work, it shows. You don't, it shows that too.
