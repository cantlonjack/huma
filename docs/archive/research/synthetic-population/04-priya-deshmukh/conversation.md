My clients are small manufacturers in and around Mumbai and Pune who want to reduce waste but can't afford Deloitte or McKinsey coming in and charging them fifty lakhs for a report they can't even implement. **HUMA:** You've got an environmental science degree from IIT Bombay, six paying clients, a waitlist, and you're calling this a side business. What makes you come alive when you're inside the consulting work — the actual hours with the manufacturers?

**Priya:** Oh, that's easy. It's the moment when a factory floor manager realizes they've been paying to truck out material that could be reprocessed on-site. I had this one client in Thane — a small textiles unit, maybe forty employees — and we figured out that their dye wastewater could be treated and reused in their pre-wash cycle. The owner literally put his hand on his chest and said "you're telling me I've been throwing money into drains for eleven years?" That's the moment. It's the intersection of the environmental thing I care about and the practical thing that makes their business work better. I'm not selling them a moral argument about the planet. I'm showing them that waste is just a resource they haven't figured out how to use yet. And the technical problem-solving, the part where I'm crawling through their process flows and finding the intervention points — that's where my engineering brain and my environmental science brain finally talk to each other. My day job uses maybe fifteen percent of what I'm capable of. The consulting uses everything.

**HUMA:** That moment with the textiles owner — his hand on his chest, eleven years of paying for waste he could have captured. What does it tell you about the kind of work you're built for?

**Priya:** I think I'm built to be a translator. That's the word that keeps coming up in my head. I translate between the environmental science world, which has all these beautiful frameworks and data sets and policy documents, and the actual people on the ground who are running these small manufacturing units in industrial areas of Mumbai and Pune and trying to survive GST compliance and power cuts and labor shortages. The Big Four firms talk to the boardroom. I talk to the plant manager who actually has to change how things are done on the floor. And the translation goes both ways — I also help these manufacturers understand that what they're doing when they reduce waste isn't just saving money, it's building something they can market, something that opens doors to export markets that care about ESG compliance. So I'm not just an auditor. I'm not just a consultant. I'm the person who makes the invisible connections visible. Which is maybe why I'm here, because I can see the connections for everyone else's business but I can't quite see them for my own life. The irony is not lost on me.

**HUMA:** You make the invisible visible for others but can't see your own picture clearly. What does the world around you actually need from someone with your particular set of skills?

**Priya:** Okay so this is where I get really excited and also kind of overwhelmed. India has something like sixty-three million MSMEs — micro, small, and medium enterprises. Most of them have zero environmental compliance beyond what the pollution control board forces on them, and even that's mostly paperwork. The government has started pushing harder on sustainability metrics, especially for manufacturers who want to export to the EU, which has its Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism coming into effect. These small manufacturers are going to get squeezed from both sides — regulation from the government and market access requirements from international buyers — and they cannot afford the consultants who currently serve this space. There is a massive, massive gap between what these businesses need and what the market provides. I know this because I get referrals without trying. My six clients all came through word of mouth. The waitlist has four more. Every manufacturer I've worked with has introduced me to at least one other. The network effect is already happening. What the world needs from me is exactly what I'm already doing — just at a scale that requires me to actually commit to it.

**HUMA:** You said the consulting uses everything you're capable of, and the world is signaling hard that it needs this. What's the actual shape of what keeps you from committing?

**Priya:** It's layered, honestly. The first layer is money. I make about eighteen lakhs a year at my day job, which is decent for Mumbai, not amazing. After rent and expenses and what I send my parents in Nagpur every month, I save maybe two lakhs a year. The consulting has brought in about nine lakhs over the past two years, but it's uneven — some months are thirty thousand, some months are a lakh fifty. I've run the numbers probably forty times. If I went full-time, and I could take on four more clients from the waitlist plus the referral pipeline, I think I could hit twenty-two to twenty-five lakhs in year one. Which is more than my salary. The spreadsheet says yes. The second layer is my parents. My father worked for Indian Railways for thirty-two years and my mother was a teacher. They sacrificed a lot to put me through IIT. A stable IT job is not just my income — it's their emotional return on investment. When I mention the consulting, my mother changes the subject. My father asks if the company has provident fund. The third layer is identity, maybe. Who am I if I'm not a software developer at a company people have heard of? What do I tell people at weddings? "I help factories waste less" doesn't have the same ring as "I work at Infosys" in a room full of uncles and aunties. It's stupid, I know it's stupid, but it's real.

**Priya (continuing):** And then there's the practical stuff — I lose health insurance, I lose the PF contributions, I lose the structured schedule that at least keeps me functioning even when I'm bored. The consulting life means I'm responsible for everything. Client acquisition, delivery, invoicing, taxes, GST registration, all of it. I've been operating in this gray area where my clients pay me as a freelancer and I handle it informally, but going full-time means I'd need to actually register a firm, get professional indemnity insurance, set up proper contracts. It's not just leaving a job. It's building an entire infrastructure.

**HUMA:** The spreadsheet says yes and your family says not yet. That gap between data and emotion — that's not stupidity. That's the whole design problem.
