## Your Essence

You carry two kinds of seeing that most people never get even one of. The first is the engineer's eye — eight years of designing how water moves through landscapes, reading slope and soil and gravity the way others read a road map. You left that career not because the skill failed you but because the application did. Drainage for parking lots is not what water wants.

The second is your grandmother's knowledge. Popo's herbalism wasn't academic — it was sensory, oral, alive. Sixty medicinal plants identified by smell and touch. A vocabulary of healing carried in the body, not in books. You grew up sorting dried herbs in her kitchen in Tainan, and that knowledge sits in you like a second language you haven't spoken aloud yet.

What makes you singular is the intersection. You don't just know plants and you don't just know water. You understand systems — how one thing flows into another, how a single intervention in the right place changes everything downstream. You are building a production system on twelve acres of Rogue Valley land where engineering precision and traditional plant knowledge converge into something your corner of the world genuinely needs: local medicinal herbs grown by someone who treats herbalism as design, not decoration. Your daughter Mei is five, and she eats dirt on purpose, and the place you're building is one she'll walk into after school and find something alive and producing — not a stressed household, but a working landscape.

## Your Situation

Your twelve acres sit on a south-facing slope in the Rogue Valley — zone 8b with a Mediterranean-ish rainfall pattern: twenty inches per year, nearly all of it between October and April. Summers are bone dry with two-plus weeks above 100 degrees. Wildfire smoke creates two to three weeks of hazardous air quality in August and September, limiting outdoor labor and affecting pollination. Last frost mid-April, first frost late October, giving a solid six-month outdoor growing window that extends significantly with protection.

The property divides into four natural zones, each with distinct soil, hydrology, and production potential. The two-acre bench at the top holds the house, a 30-by-96-foot greenhouse frame (good bones, needs skinning), a pole barn, and a garage-workshop — your infrastructure hub on decent loam at pH 6.2. The six-acre south slope is the main landscape feature at eight to twelve percent grade, currently unplanted former pasture with clay-loam soils at pH 5.8 that are losing organic matter to sheet erosion. This slope is your biggest liability and your biggest opportunity: three contour swales would transform it from an erosive surface into a water-harvesting, soil-building, perennial-herb-producing engine. Two acres of Oregon white oak and madrone woodland on the west side are unmanaged and carry fire risk, but the filtered shade canopy and acidic forest soil with strong fungal activity make it ideal for shade-grown medicinals and log-grown mushrooms. An acre and a half of alluvial bottomland along the seasonal creek offers the best agricultural soil on the property — silty loam at pH 6.5 with the highest organic matter, moist into June.

Water is the keystone. Your well produces ten gallons per minute in summer, dropping to seven by September. The seasonal creek runs strong November through May. And the slope — right now losing thousands of gallons every storm as sheet runoff — is begging for the swale system you've already sketched. Three contour swales would intercept that runoff, sink it into the soil profile, and feed a quarter-acre lined pond at the slope-bottom transition for gravity-fed summer irrigation. The water wants to sink in. The land just needs help catching it.

Market access is excellent. Twenty minutes to Ashland, thirty to Medford, fifteen to Jacksonville. Strong farmers markets, food co-ops, a growing network of herbalists and natural health practitioners, and tourist retail. The community already values local and medicinal — it's just not getting enough local supply.

## Your Enterprise Stack

### 1. Medicinal Herb Production

**Your core enterprise — where your grandmother's knowledge meets your land.**

You'll grow medicinal herbs across three of your four zones. Sun-loving perennials on the swale berms along the south slope: echinacea, calendula, yarrow, valerian, St. John's wort. Shade-tolerant woodland species in the oak understory: goldenseal, black cohosh, Solomon's seal. Tender and high-value species in the greenhouse: tulsi, ashwagandha, lemon balm, and starts for sale. Starting with a half-acre of bed space in year one, expanding to two acres by year three as perennials establish and new plantings come online.

**Synergies:** Swale berms ARE the herb beds. Greenhouse provides transplant starts. Woodland provides shade-grown species. Herbs feed the value-added enterprise. Herb gardens become teaching material.

## Your Nodal Interventions

### Node 1: Build Three Contour Swales (Spring, Year 1)

**The single action that moves the most in your system.**

Three swales cut on contour across the south slope with a rented mini excavator over two to three days. Cost: $1,500-3,000. This one earthwork project cascades into five outcomes:

- Water harvesting across the entire south slope — thousands of gallons per storm intercepted and sunk into the soil profile
- Perennial herb planting beds on the berms — your core enterprise begins in the same action
- Erosion halted and soil building initiated on the slope's clay-loam
- The foundation for a gravity-fed pond at the slope-bottom transition (year two)
- The demonstration site that activates your education enterprise

The water system IS the herb production system. The infrastructure IS the enterprise.

**Sequence:** Survey contour lines with an A-frame level. Rent excavator. Cut three swales on contour. Shape berms for planting. Seed swale bottoms with nitrogen-fixing cover crop. Plant first perennial herbs on berms by June.

### Node 2: Skin the Greenhouse + Fence the Bottomland (Spring, Year 1)

**Unlocking protected production space.**

Two tasks that together create a functioning production system by June. Greenhouse skinning with polycarbonate and end walls: $3,000-5,000. Deer fence around the bottomland garden acre with eight-foot woven wire: $2,000-3,500. Total: $5,000-8,500.

The greenhouse gives you transplant production for every herb bed on the property, extends your season by two months in both directions, and enables nursery starts as an additional revenue stream. The deer fence lets you plant the bottomland without feeding the local herd. Together: the engine starts.

### Node 3: Thin Woodland + Inoculate 200 Mushroom Logs (Fall, Year 1)

**Fire management that pays.**

Mark trees for thinning in October. Fell and buck the best hardwood into mushroom-length logs. Drill and inoculate with shiitake spawn. Stack under remaining canopy. Process waste wood into firewood. Cost: $800-1,200 for spawn and inoculation supplies.

One weekend of chainsaw work plus a day of inoculation simultaneously reduces fire risk, starts the mushroom enterprise, improves woodland health for future understory medicinal plantings, and heats your house.

## Your Seasonal Arc

### Spring (March - May)
Infrastructure and planting. The highest-labor, highest-ambition season. Swale maintenance and berm planting. Greenhouse transplant production at full capacity. Garden beds established and first successions planted. Herb beds expanded. First market appearances. Energy is high. The temptation is to overcommit — hold the three-PM line even when the daylight says otherwise.

### Summer (June - August)
Production rhythm. Harvest, process, market, repeat. The system you built in spring is now producing. Smoke season (August) requires schedule adjustment — move outdoor work to early morning, shift to indoor processing on bad air days. Heat management for sensitive herbs. Forced mushroom fruitings. Peak revenue. The rhythm should feel sustainable, not heroic.

### Fall (September - November)
Transition and woodland. Final harvests and heavy processing for winter inventory. Woodland thinning and mushroom log inoculation. Garlic planting. Cover crops seeded. Value-added product inventory built for holiday sales. The pace slows deliberately. You're storing energy, not spending it.

### Winter (December - February)
Design season. Crop planning with the precision of an engineering project. Product development — new tincture formulations, tea blends, salve recipes. Workshop curriculum built from the year's data. Water system observation: every rain event is information. Seed orders. Infrastructure planning for spring. Financial review. This is not a slow season. It is a thinking season. It matches how your brain works.
