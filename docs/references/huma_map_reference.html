Who You Are

Sarah is a builder who works through living systems. She trained as an environmental engineer, spent eight years in municipal water infrastructure, and walked away when she realized she was designing drainage for parking lots instead of watersheds. What she carries from that work is a precise eye for how water moves through a landscape — a skill that most beginning operators spend years developing and she already has in her bones.

She comes alive in two modes: the quiet of early morning observation — walking the land, reading what changed overnight, noticing where the deer bedded and where the frost sat longest — and the intensity of building, hands in soil or hands on tools, shaping something tangible from raw material. She has no patience for abstraction that doesn't become action.

Her community in the Rogue Valley is hungry for what she wants to offer. There's a growing cohort of young families who want local food but no farmer within twenty miles doing direct-to-consumer. The Saturday market in Ashland is underserved in pastured protein and year-round greens. She has social capital she hasn't fully seen yet: her neighbors already trust her, her former engineering colleagues have started asking when they can buy eggs, and her partner teaches at the community college — a built-in network for education enterprises.

Your Land

Twelve acres in the Rogue Valley at 1,400 feet, on a gentle south-southwest slope that catches winter sun and sheds cold air to the valley floor below. This is Zone 7b — moderate winters, hot dry summers, 220-day growing season. The land has been in hay production for the last fifteen years, which means the soil is compacted below six inches but not chemically degraded. There's biology to work with.

The most striking feature is the water. A year-round spring emerges at the northeast corner, currently flowing uncaptured into a drainage ditch that exits the property. Sarah's engineering eye immediately saw what this spring could become: a gravity-fed system serving every zone of the operation without pumping costs. This is the highest-leverage feature on the property and the single most important element to design around.

Three mature Oregon white oaks anchor the western boundary, providing shade, wildlife habitat, and the beginning of a silvopasture corridor. An old equipment barn sits near the road frontage — structurally sound, needing new roofing. Access is good: paved county road to the gate, gravel drive to the barn, but no internal farm roads yet for paddock access.
